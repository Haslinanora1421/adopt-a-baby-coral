What Is Coral Adoption?

Coral reef adoption introduces a groundbreaking opportunity for people of all ages and backgrounds to actively engage in the vital mission of rehabilitating and conserving our precious coral reefs. With the convenience of participating from the comfort of your own home, this innovative initiative ensures that anyone, regardless of location or experience, can make a meaningful impact on ocean conservation without the need for direct sea involvement.

Why Consider Coral Adoption? Dive into a World of Impact!

Coral adoption isn't just a choice; it's a transformative journey towards preserving the very essence of our oceans. By embracing coral adoption, you become a steward of marine life, safeguarding fragile ecosystems and ensuring a vibrant future for generations to come. Join the movement today and discover the power you hold to make waves of change, right from your own home.

How does it Work?

Grab Your Benefit!

Coral adopters can enjoy several benefits from their participation. Some common benefits typically provided to adopters include:

Certificate

Coral adopters will be provided with a certificate as recognition for their contribution to coral conservation. This certificate can be used as evidence of their participation and support for conservation efforts.

Nametag

A nametag is usually given to coral adopters. This can be used when visiting the adopted coral site or attending coral conservation-related events. Nametags help identify adopters and reinforce their sense of identity as part of the coral conservation effort.

Location Map

A location map is a useful tool for coral adopters to visually understand the exact location of the adopted coral site. This map provides information about the geographical location and coral reef. It can also help adopters gain a deeper understanding of the broader coral ecosystem.

Monthly Reports

Monthly reports on coral conditions provide up-to-date information on the development and status of the adopted coral reef. These reports may include data on coral health, species found, threats faced, and conservation activities undertaken. The reports help adopters stay connected to the coral conservation project they support and understand the impact of their participation.

- Acropora -

Acropora muricata

Branches like twigs

IDR 150K-750K

Colonies are arborescent, with cylindrical branches. They usually form thickets and may form single species stands over 10 metres across. In shallow water branches are short and compact, while in deeper water colonies have more open branches. Axial corallites are exsert. Radial corallites are tubular. They may be similar or varied in size, and uniformly or irregularly distributed.

Acropora hyacinthus

Creeping form, like rudimentary Acropora

IDR 150K-750K

Colonies are wide flat plates and tables or tiered aggregations of small plates. Branchlets are thin and finely structured except where exposed to wave action, when they become closely fused. Branchlets are fine and upward-projecting. Axial corallites are distinct but not exsert. Radial corallites are cup-shaped and form rosettes.

Acropora palifera

Plate-branched and sturdy

IDR 150K-750K

Isopora palifera is a species of stony coral in the family Acroporidae. It is a reef building coral living in shallow water and adopts different forms depending on the water conditions where it is situated. It is found in the Western Indo-Pacific Ocean as far east as Australia.

Acropora humilis

Branches are close together like fingers

IDR 150K-750K

The species is found in digitate colonies of thick tapering branches. The branches contain one or more axial corallites that are large and dome-shaped, and either incipient axial corallites or branchlets at their bases. Its radial corallites are found in two different diameters, the larger of which are organised in rows.

Montipora informis

Horizontal branching

IDR 150K-750K

Colonies are massive to encrusting. Corallites are evenly distributed and immersed. The coenosteum is covered with elongate papillae of uniform length. Their colours are brown or mottled brown and white. Papillae may have white or blue tips. White tentacles may be extended during the day.

- Non-Acropora -

Leptoseris yabei

The shape resembles a sheet

IDR 150K-750K

Colonies are laminar, in whorls or tiers or vase-shaped. They are commonly over one metre across. Corallites are enclosed in rectangular pockets formed between radiating ridges and low walls which are parallel to frond margins. Septo-costae are moderately exsert and alternate.

Fungia concinna

Mushroom-like shape, solitary

IDR 150K-750K

Polyps are circular, up to 160 millimetres diameter and generally flat. Septa are densely packed. Septal teeth and costal spines are small, giving septa a smooth appearance. Tentacular lobes are not formed or are minute. The undersurface is usually without pits.

Millepora dichotoma

All types of fire coral, yellow at the end of the colony

IDR 150K-750K

M. dichotoma is a colonial hermatypic coral with a calcareous endoskeleton. They form colonies up to 60 cm wide, but clumps of colonies may be several meters across. They initially build as encrusting coral, adhering themselves to hard substrate.

Heliopora coerulea

Blue coral, the presence of blue on the skeleton

IDR 150K-750K

Blue coral is a species of colonial coral. It is the only octocoral known to produce a massive skeleton. This skeleton is formed of aragonite, similar to that of scleractinia. Individual polyps live in tubes within the skeleton and are connected by a thin layer of tissue over the outside of the skeleton.

- Price -

Coral Fragment

IDR 150K

If you're just getting into the hobby you may be hearing this term tossed around a lot. Frags are those small pieces of coral being sold at aquarium stores and many of the reefing events. Frag is short for the fragment you are cutting or breaking off a coral colony.

Coral Spider

IDR 750K

The damaged coral reef’s ability to regenerate will be improved with the installation of coral spiders. The outcome of this project will be the initial transformation of a coral rubble field into an area covered by living coral fragments, growing over time and restoring the natural reef ecosystem.
